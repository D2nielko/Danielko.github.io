I kept seeing "Kelly criterion" mentioned in quant interview prep threads
without a real explanation, so I derived it and then simulated it to see
why practitioners always talk about "half-Kelly" instead of the real thing.

## The setup

Suppose you have a bet that wins with probability $p$ and pays out $b$-to-1
(you stake $1, win $b$ if you're right, lose your $1 if you're wrong), and
you can bet a fraction $f$ of your bankroll each round. Let $q = 1 - p$.
The Kelly criterion says the growth-optimal fraction to bet is:

$$
f^* = p - \frac{q}{b}
$$

## Where it comes from

The idea is to maximize the expected log of your wealth, not the expected
wealth itself. If you bet fraction $f$, after one round your bankroll
multiplies by $(1 + fb)$ on a win or $(1 - f)$ on a loss. Expected log
growth per round is:

$$
g(f) = p \log(1 + fb) + q \log(1 - f)
$$

Taking the derivative and setting it to zero:

$$
g'(f) = \frac{pb}{1+fb} - \frac{q}{1-f} = 0
$$

Solving for $f$ gives exactly $f^* = p - q/b$. Maximizing log-wealth instead
of raw expected wealth is the whole trick — it's what keeps you from
betting everything on a favorable coin flip, because $\log(0)$ is
$-\infty$ and the optimizer knows it.

## Why full Kelly still hurts

Full Kelly maximizes long-run growth rate, but it says nothing about the
variance along the way. In practice your estimate of $p$ is never exact,
and Kelly sizing is brutally sensitive to overestimating your edge — bet
as if $p$ is 5 points higher than it really is and you can turn a
positive-growth strategy into a losing one.

I simulated 500 sequences of 200 bets each, at $p = 0.55$, $b = 1$
(so $f^* = 0.10$), comparing full Kelly to half Kelly ($f^*/2$):

```python
import numpy as np

def simulate(f, p=0.55, b=1.0, rounds=200, trials=500, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    wins = rng.random((trials, rounds)) < p
    growth = np.where(wins, np.log(1 + f * b), np.log(1 - f))
    log_wealth = growth.cumsum(axis=1)
    return log_wealth[:, -1]

f_full = 0.55 - 0.45 / 1.0  # 0.10
final_full = simulate(f_full)
final_half = simulate(f_full / 2)

print("full Kelly  : mean", final_full.mean(), "std", final_full.std())
print("half Kelly  : mean", final_half.mean(), "std", final_half.std())
```

On a run I did locally, full Kelly's mean final log-wealth was noticeably
higher, but its standard deviation across trials was almost double half
Kelly's. Half Kelly gives up roughly a quarter of the growth rate (it's a
known result that betting $cf^*$ gives growth $\approx c(2-c)$ times the
full-Kelly growth, so $c=0.5$ keeps 75%) for a much smoother ride.

## The practical caveat

This is the part that matters more than the formula: $f^*$ is only correct
if you actually know $p$ and $b$. You don't. Any real edge estimate has
error bars, and because the log-growth curve is concave and drops off
sharply on the over-betting side, the cost of overestimating your edge is
much worse than the cost of underestimating it. That asymmetry is the
entire reason "fractional Kelly" (half or even quarter) is the practical
default — it trades a bit of theoretical growth for a lot of robustness to
being wrong about your own edge.
